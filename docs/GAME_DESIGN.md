# Chronicle AI

## Vision

Chronicle AI is an exploratory tabletop RPG presented like a warm, pixel-art adventure game. Playing should feel like setting out across a world that remembers you: wandering roads, entering towns, uncovering secrets, talking to strange people, solving problems creatively, and facing danger through clear, deterministic mechanics.

The inspirations are familiar: Pokemon's readable exploration and discovery, Sea of Stars' premium RPG presentation, Golden Sun's sense of magical journey, Octopath Traveler's travelogue structure, classic Zelda's curiosity-driven worlds, and D&D's freedom to try almost anything. Chronicle AI is not trying to copy any one of them. It combines overworld exploration, tabletop freedom, AI storytelling, deterministic rules, and persistent worlds into one RPG experience.

The AI Director is there to make the world feel responsive and alive. It adapts story, dialogue, texture, and consequence around the player. It does not replace game systems. The player should feel that their choices matter because the world records them, the rules resolve them, and the story grows around them.

Chronicle AI should be approachable enough for a first-time solo adventurer and deep enough for a long-running campaign. It should support heroic journeys, mysteries, dungeon expeditions, monster hunts, diplomacy, survival stories, and personal character arcs without forcing every campaign into the same plot shape.

## Core Gameplay Loop

The core loop is:

Explore

Discover

Interact

Dialogue

Choice

Skill Check / Combat / Puzzle

Reward

World Changes

Continue Exploring

Exploration is the default state. The player moves through an overworld, town, dungeon, cave, ruin, road, or interior space and notices something interesting. Discovery creates a reason to act: an NPC, a locked door, a suspicious shrine, a shortcut, a monster sign, a rumor, a hidden chest, a faction conflict, or a strange environmental clue.

Interaction turns discovery into play. The player talks, investigates, fights, bargains, sneaks, solves, helps, steals, repairs, explores, or retreats. The game responds with dialogue, choice, skill checks, combat, puzzles, rewards, and world changes. Those changes become part of the campaign and create the next reason to explore.

## Player Fantasy

The player is a wandering adventurer whose choices shape the world around them. Depending on the campaign, they may be a dungeon explorer, detective, monster hunter, diplomat, folk hero, scholar, treasure seeker, survivor, or reluctant champion.

Chronicle AI should support multiple identities without forcing a single heroic script. A player can solve problems with steel, wit, stealth, compassion, leverage, or curiosity. The AI adapts the story around the player's approach rather than dragging them down a fixed narrative corridor.

The fantasy is not just "be told a story." It is "step into a world, make choices, and have the world remember."

## Pillars

- Player agency: Players should be able to attempt creative actions and receive grounded outcomes.
- Living world: Places, NPCs, factions, and consequences should persist and evolve.
- Exploration first: Movement, discovery, and curiosity drive the experience.
- Consequences matter: Choices should leave traces in relationships, quests, locations, and reputation.
- Tactical combat: Combat should be readable, risky, and mechanically honest.
- Rich companions: Important allies should feel persistent, opinionated, and changed by the journey.
- Secrets reward curiosity: Players who inspect, revisit, listen, and experiment should find meaningful discoveries.
- AI supports, not replaces, game systems: AI adds narration and responsiveness while rules and data remain authoritative.

## World

Chronicle AI worlds are made of regions, roads, towns, dungeons, landmarks, interiors, hidden places, factions, weather, and time. The world should invite wandering. A mountain on the horizon, a ruined tower, a ferry crossing, a shrine in the woods, or a locked cellar should all feel like invitations.

Regions create identity and scale. Towns provide people, rest, trade, rumors, politics, and emotional stakes. Dungeons provide pressure, danger, puzzles, secrets, and rewards. Landmarks help players orient themselves and remember the journey. Weather and time should support atmosphere and gameplay only when they are real parts of the world, not decorative claims.

Factions give the world memory beyond individual NPCs. A choice in one place can ripple outward through reputation, access, prices, hostility, protection, or rumors.

## Character Progression

Progression should make the player feel more capable without erasing risk. Leveling improves survivability, skill, and tactical options. Equipment changes what a character can attempt and how they approach danger. Classes and traits shape identity, but the game should leave room for custom character concepts.

Skills should reward different playstyles: force, finesse, endurance, reasoning, perception, influence, and future campaign-specific specialties. Titles and reputation should reflect what the world believes about the character. Companions should grow through shared experiences, not merely unlock as collectibles.

Progression should be readable. The player should understand why they improved, what changed, and how that affects future choices.

## Exploration

Exploration should feel like Pokemon routes meeting Zelda spaces: readable paths, visible points of interest, optional detours, hidden rewards, and a steady rhythm of curiosity. The player should wonder, "What is over there?" and the game should often have an answer.

The overworld gives physical shape to travel. Interiors, caves, ruins, forests, towers, and underground spaces create local challenges and secrets. Puzzles should reward observation and experimentation rather than opaque guessing. Shortcuts should make the world feel connected. Hidden areas should make revisiting worthwhile.

Movement itself can be calm, but discovery should be frequent. Not every tile needs meaning; every place should have the possibility of meaning.

## Dialogue

Dialogue should feel like talking to people who belong to the world. AI dialogue allows NPCs to respond naturally, but memory and consequence make those conversations matter. NPCs should remember important choices, promises, insults, favors, fears, and shared danger.

Player choices should include more than obvious good and evil. Good dialogue creates tension between truth, kindness, leverage, curiosity, and risk. Some conversations should solve problems. Others should create new ones.

Relationships should change because of what the player does, not because the AI decides a dramatic turn would be convenient.

## Combat

Combat should be tactical, readable, and paced like an RPG battle rather than a rules spreadsheet. The player should understand whose turn it is, what options exist, what is at risk, and why an outcome happened.

Positioning, abilities, equipment, conditions, enemy behavior, and encounter context should matter. Ordinary encounters should be quick but meaningful. Bosses should have identity, escalation, and memorable mechanics. Victory should produce aftermath: safety, loot, XP, reputation, story consequences, or new danger.

Combat is not the only solution, but when it happens it should feel fair, tense, and worth paying attention to.

## NPCs

NPCs should behave like inhabitants, not vending machines for exposition. They remember the player, react to world events, change over time, and may move between locations when the story or world systems support it.

Important NPCs should have desires, fears, loyalties, routines, and boundaries. Minor NPCs should still feel grounded in place. NPCs can lie, misunderstand, help, refuse, forgive, betray, or grow, but lasting changes must be supported by game state.

## Quests

Quests should support handcrafted arcs, AI-generated opportunities, procedural discoveries, and branching outcomes. A quest is not just a task list; it is a promise that the world will respond when the player acts.

Good quests can begin from rumors, exploration, NPC needs, faction pressure, strange objects, visible threats, or player curiosity. They should allow multiple approaches when possible. Completion should change something: a person, a place, a relationship, a faction, a reward, or the player's understanding of the world.

## Inventory

Inventory should feel like the character's carried life: weapons, armor, tools, supplies, treasures, documents, keys, trophies, and oddities. Items should matter because they create options.

The game should avoid inventory noise. Important items should be readable, useful, memorable, or valuable. Equipment should support character identity and tactical choice. Quest items should connect clearly to the world.

## Economy

The economy should make towns, travel, rewards, and scarcity meaningful. Gold and trade should support choices: buy better gear, save for rare supplies, bribe a guard, repair equipment, fund a journey, or invest in relationships.

Prices should feel grounded in place and reputation. The economy should never become more important than adventure, but it should make rewards and losses feel real.

## Crafting

Crafting should reward exploration and preparation. Materials found in the world can become potions, tools, upgrades, camp supplies, charms, repairs, or special quest solutions.

Crafting should be understandable and grounded. Recipes and requirements should create goals, not chores. The best crafting outcomes should feel like the player learned the world and used it well.

## Reputation

Reputation is how the world remembers the player beyond individual quests. Towns, factions, companions, and NPCs may form opinions based on mercy, violence, honesty, betrayal, heroism, greed, reliability, or fear.

Reputation should create opportunities and consequences: doors open, prices shift, guards watch closely, allies volunteer, enemies prepare, rumors spread, and old choices return in new forms.

## Companion System

Companions should feel like people who travel with the player, not bonus stats with portraits. They have memories, opinions, fears, skills, and personal stakes. They may support the player, challenge them, leave, return, disagree, or grow.

Companions should deepen exploration, dialogue, and combat. They should create moments of care, conflict, humor, and consequence. Their presence must remain grounded in persistent state.

## Creator Tools

Creator tools should let players and game masters build campaigns without needing to become software developers. A creator should be able to define a premise, tone, locations, factions, NPCs, encounters, quests, reference documents, and constraints.

The tools should encourage structure where it matters and freedom where it helps. Creators provide the bones of the world; the AI Director helps bring it to life during play while respecting those boundaries.

## Accessibility

Accessibility is part of game design, not only interface compliance. Players should be able to understand choices, read outcomes, recover from mistakes, and play at a comfortable pace.

The game should support clear text, readable contrast, reduced motion, visible focus, understandable controls, transparent rolls, explicit consequences, and alternatives to speed-based interaction. Mystery is welcome; confusion is not.

## Long-Term Vision

In five or more years, Chronicle AI should be a platform for persistent RPG worlds. Players can start solo adventures, join friends, create campaigns, publish worlds, import reference material, build towns and dungeons, shape factions, travel with companions, and return months later to a world that remembers.

The dream is a living library of playable tabletop worlds: some intimate and handcrafted, some sprawling and procedural, all grounded in deterministic mechanics and persistent memory. AI makes the world responsive; design makes it coherent; rules make it fair.

# Design Principles

Every gameplay feature should improve at least one of these:

- Exploration
- Discovery
- Agency
- Immersion
- Replayability
- Storytelling
- Tactical depth

If it improves none of them, it probably does not belong.
